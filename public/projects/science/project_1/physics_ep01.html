Section One

What Is Motion, Really?

At its core, motion is simply the change in position of an object over time. First, we track where an object is: \(x(t)\). Then we ask how quickly that position changes ( the speed ): \(v(t) = \frac{dx}{dt}\). Finally, we ask what changes that change: acceleration \(a(t) = \frac{dv}{dt} = \frac{d^2x}{dt^2}\). The key revolutionary step in Newtonian mechanics is connecting acceleration to causes through force.

The Kinematic Equations — Constant Acceleration

When acceleration is constant (like near Earth's surface), four equations link position, velocity, acceleration and time. These were derived by Galileo through inclined-plane experiments long before Newton:

$$v = v_0 + at$$

Velocity after time t

$$x = x_0 + v_0 t + \tfrac{1}{2}at^2$$

Position after time t

$$v^2 = v_0^2 + 2a(x - x_0)$$

Velocity—displacement relation

$$x = x_0 + \tfrac{1}{2}(v + v_0)t$$

Average velocity formula

Conservation Laws

Noether's theorem reveals that conservation laws are not coincidences — they are symmetry. The universe's homogeneity in space means momentum is conserved; its homogeneity in time means energy is conserved.

Work — force over displacement$W = \int F \cdot dx$
Kinetic energy$E_c = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$
Gravitational potential energy$E_p = mgh$
Conservation of mechanical energy$E_m = E_c + E_p = \text{const}$
Rollercoaster — Ec / Ep exchange
Energy distribution at each point
Conservation of Momentumi
$$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}, \quad \frac{d\vec{p}}{dt} = \vec{F}_{\text{ext}}$$

In an isolated system ($\vec{F}_{\text{ext}} = 0$), total momentum is constant. This is why rockets work: ejecting mass backward creates forward momentum.

Conservation of Energyi
$$E_m = \frac{1}{2}mv^2 + mgh = \text{const}$$

Kinetic energy $T = \frac{1}{2}mv^2$ plus potential energy $V$ is constant when only conservative forces act. This underlies roller coasters, planetary orbits, and pendulum clocks.

Conservation of Angular Momentumi
$$\vec{L} = \vec{r} \times \vec{p}, \quad \frac{d\vec{L}}{dt} = \vec{M}$$

When no external torque acts, angular momentum is conserved. This is why figure skaters spin faster by pulling in their arms — reducing $r$ increases $\omega$ to keep $L$ constant.

— Energy Conservation — Pendulum

A pendulum of length $\ell$ released from angle $\theta_0$ exchanges kinetic and potential energy continuously. The maximum speed at the bottom: $v_{\max} = \sqrt{2g\ell(1-\cos\theta_0)}$

Noether's Theorem
$\frac{\partial L}{\partial t} = 0 \implies \frac{dE}{dt} = 0$

Energy conservation is not an axiom. It follows from time-translation symmetry — the laws of physics don't change with time.

Deterministic Chaos

Sensitive dependence on initial conditions

If nature is deterministic, then why is the future so hard to predict? From the rigid laws of motion, a new behavior emerges—one where the tiniest difference in the present can lead to a completely different future.

Two double pendulums · Δθ = 0.001°

Deterministic · not random · completely unpredictable

# Double pendulum — Newtonian sim

def derivatives(state, t):
  theta1, omega1, theta2, omega2 = state
  dtheta = theta2 - theta1

  # Coupled ODEs
  dtheta1 = omega1
  dtheta2 = omega2
  domega1 = (m2*g*sin(theta2)*cos(dtheta)
        - m2*sin(dtheta)*(l1*omega1**2*cos(dtheta)
        + l2*omega2**2) - (m1+m2)*g*sin(theta1))
        / (l1*(m1 + m2*sin(dtheta)**2))

  return [dtheta1, dtheta2, domega1, domega2]

# Two nearly-identical starting states
state_A = [np.pi/2, 0, np.pi/2, 0]
state_B = [np.pi/2 + 1e-5, 0, np.pi/2, 0]

# Diverge completely within seconds
sol = odeint(derivatives, state_A, t)

Rigid Body Rotation

i

Rotational analogs of Newton's laws use moment of inertia $I = \int r^2\,dm$ instead of mass: $$\tau = I\alpha, \qquad L = I\omega, \qquad KE_{\text{rot}} = \tfrac{1}{2}I\omega^2$$

3D rotating objects - different mass distributions, different moments of inertia

Parallel Axis Theorem

If $I_{\text{cm}}$ is the moment of inertia about an axis through the center of mass, then about any parallel axis displaced by distance $d$:

$$I = I_{\text{cm}} + Md^2$$

This lets us compute $I$ about any axis if we know $I_{\text{cm}}$. For a uniform rod: $I_{\text{cm}} = ML^2/12$ about the center; $I_{\text{end}} = ML^2/3$ about one end. The Perpendicular Axis Theorem adds: for a flat lamina, $I_z = I_x + I_y$.
